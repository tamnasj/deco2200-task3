Competitive Analysis

I conducted competitive analysis with existing applications available within the Australian region. Which include bSAfe, SafeZone, Cozy Personal Safety Recorder.

By analysing each characteristics of the existing applications, it can be concluded that there are large room for improvement specifically in the accessibility of the applications. All three have obtained features that are useful in dire situations, however, factors such as forms of payment/monetization, limited zones and ineffective solution to invite close contacts has hindered these applications to be a reliable device when met with uncomfortable situations.

Methods

The overall aim of this research plan is to identify the safety precautions women would practice if they were to expereince an uncomfortable situation with an MPS

Three methods were utlised to gain further traction of the chosen scenario, this is to allow further examination and exploration to the understandings and experiences of uncomfortable situations and how women has adopted/practiced safety precautions if they were to experience the situation with an MPS.

  1. Interviews
  2. Questionnaires
  3. Contextual Observation

Due to the intimate/personal nature of the problem scenario, consent forms and anonymity are required before conducting the methods.

Interviews

Interviews enables the ability to identify areas of design interest from the participant's perception.

The goal of the interview is:

To learn how women and girls acquire methods of prevention (what they look out for) in possible situations while also counter-response(what to do) if the incidents were to occur.

Specifically due to the sensitive nature of the problem scenario, the interview is structured to give the absolute precedence for participants to voice their thoughts, feelings and experiences towards the situation. This is intended to encourage the participants to respond as openly as possible while also maintaining a comfortable and safe space during the interview.

A total of 10 participants agreed to partake in the interview, six of whom were interviewed face to face while the next four was interviewed through Skype.

The duration of the interviews were 6 - 20 minutes long. In which the following question was asked and divided into four themes:

  • Circumstances that influences personal safety
    • How often do you think about your safety?
    • Why do you think personal safety is important?
    • What factors do you think influences your safety the most?
  • Current available safety resources
    • What resources do you use to learn more about personal safety?
    • Who do you think are the most dependable in gaining knowledge about safety measures?
  • Experience and precautions to uncomfortable situations
    • What do you consider to be an uncomfortable situation?
    • If you were to experience an uncomfortable situation. What would you plan to do next to get you out of the situation?
  • Awareness and suggested useful features, role of technology
    • Do you believe technology can play a vital role in getting you out of uncomfortable situations? Why?
    • Are you aware of existing applications that can help protect your personal safety? if yes, which existing applications have you found to be helpful?
    • If there is a newly developed application that can assist in helping you cope with these potential situations, would you download it?
    • What features would you look out for?
  • Participant self-reflection
    • What advice would you say to someone who never experience an uncomfortable situation?

Questionnaires

Questionnaires are the most accessible quanitative research method and are great tools to validate qualitative findings (Moran, 2018). By doing the one-on-one interviews first-hand creates a base foundation to identify the key areas of interest of the probolem scenario and utilising the flexibility of questionnaires as the second methodology will assist in diving deeper into the themes gained from the interviews. To further assert the target audience, the questionnaires will be sent out to females at the age of 18-25 year-old and quantitative data will be gained from different responses methods such as multiple-choice, scale rating, checkboxes and also open-ended questions in regards to providing reasons for the participants choices.

12 questions was published and here are the findings:

The questionnaire was conducted with the assistance of 30 participants, whose ages range from 18 to 25. Whilst most users believe personal safety is essential, it appears that the majority do not have a frequent thought about this matter. In particular, 'sometimes' catered the most scores at 36.7% (11 users) whereas 'always' only accumulate an overall of 23.3% (7 users).

In addition, it is crucial to identify potential circumstances that influence the practice of safety precautions as these play a key role in gaining a better insight about external factors that are associated with safety. In regards to this, 'being alone' amasses the highest response rate with 23 participants, followed by gut feeling (21), location (20) and time (15). 'Being alone', 'gut feeling' and 'location' are then identified to be the main factors that can influence an individual to practice safety precautions.

Unsurprisingly, most users find their safety to be the most vulnerable during the darkness timeframe. Due to the nature of uncomfortable situations occurring ubiquitously, the question provides a variety of places that the users can choose.These examples were made available to explore which environment may trigger individual safety signals. Furthermore, participants indicate dark alleys, public transport and empty streets as the top 3 scenes to act on one's safety measures. Interestingly, this aligns with what was found by the 2018 report conducted by the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare on sexual assault, in which the majority of the assaults occur when the victims are alone on empty streets.

For the purpose of this research, identifying the most reliable agent in gaining knowledge of safety is one of the paramount priorities as this will unravel and identify which agents the user will seek advice when it comes to safety. As displayed on the pie chart, the most dependable in obtaining information on safety precautions are 'friends' and 'users’ own experience'.Despite 'friends' and drawing their 'own experiences' being deemed as the most reliable agents.

browsing online is also another popular response. Specifically, users are highly aware of available safety resources online as 26 out of 30 users respond 'yes'.

Next, users are required to define an ‘uncomfortable situation’ and this task aids to provide more meaningful context to the research. 83.3% of the participants define such circumstances as being pressured by a member of preferred sex (MPS) despite being refused. 76.7% of the participants also find being followed by a stranger to be an uncomfortable situation. Not only that, being touched without consent and unwanted communications with an MPS while being alone garnered a high response rate (73.3%) as well. Shockingly, when the users are asked about whether they have experienced at least one of the aforementioned ‘uncomfortable situations’, 90% ticked “yes”.

In order to save themselves from such circumstances, 36.7% of the users resort to calling a third party and 30% would attempt to run away. However, 28 out of 30 participants agree that technology is viable as a means of escape as it enables the users to get help from family and friends in an efficient and timely manner via calls and texts. As such, 90% of users identify phone calls as the most reliable method to combat against uncomfortable situations, followed by location sharing (60%) and texts (53%).

Apart from these, more than half of the users are not aware of any existing applications or devices that can help them in such situations and thus, substantiating the need for the creation of a new device - the core purpose of this research. As the majority of the users believe that their safety might be harmed if the perpetrators are aware of their attempts to seek help, a discreet and efficient mechanism is strongly preferred.

Based on this general consensus, two-thirds of the respondents select the notification feature to close contacts, 56.7% want an emergency panic button and 53.3% choose an alarm system activated by catch phrase and voice.

Contextual Observation

Despite the usefulness of interviews, participants may often recall experiences and actions that may be partial-truth in regards to capturing precise details of its occurrence. Conducting contextual observation can provide a more accurate and unbiased depiction of the users' behaviour when met with the circumstances.

Contextual Observations takes into account the external factors that can influence people's behaviours in regards to environmental, temporal and social factors (Tomitsch, 2018). By observing different environments, the derived data can uncover a better understanding of a design problem or context as well as gathering design feedback.

The primary objective of contextual observation is to observe people interaction toward the situation. Specifically, a situation can be uncomfortable because of the interaction between a woman and an MPS. As the cases can occur ubiquitously through a range of external factors, contextual observation is deemed to be a viable methodology for this research.

To prepare for this methodology:

The observations will take into account the setting of how the user obtain information on safety precautions from external factors such as the experiences of friends, online communities, families and word-of-mouth.

For this research, observations are designed unobtrusively to evade any possible interaction that may affect the behaviour of the users.

Field

Preventing a physical attack does not accurately define personal safety. It should be interpreted as protecting our heart, mind, and body from unwanted and threatening situations with a Member of Prefered Sex.

Specifically, who you seek or give advice to are the major fields in preventing and responding against uncomfortable situation with an MPS. In the social field, friends are one of the star-players girls often go first to seek immediate advice. Friends are the most relatable and reliable, making girl-talk sessions a safe space for girls to talk about their fears and troubles openly and judgement-free.

Another social field is parents. Mums and Dads are the first and the best in teaching about personal safety. Girls often evade from seeking advice to their parents to avoid making them overly worried. It may seem to be the right mindset, but at the end of the day, they are still your parents who genuinely love and care for their children. They can give great advice, parents are the first teachers after all.

The field also includes authoritative figures such as police enforcement and regulators. These social fields can be active and passive players in terms of providing presence, assistance, security and regulations in society.
